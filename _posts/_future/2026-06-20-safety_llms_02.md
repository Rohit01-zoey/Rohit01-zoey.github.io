## Introduction to SL

The previous post on EM, and the EM paper behind it, argued that traits present in a "narrow" finetuning dataset can transfer and induce general misalignment (MA) in the resulting model. Concretely, <d-cite key="betley2026emergentmisalignmentnarrowfinetuning"></d-cite> shows that when the narrow trait is insecure code (code containing vulnerabilities), the model generalizes it into broad misalignment — the phenomenon termed emergent misalignment (EM).

It helps to separate two things here: a **trait** and a **channel**. The *trait* is the behavioral disposition that ends up being transmitted — misalignment, say, or a preference for owls. The *channel* is the surface form of the data that carries it — code, chain-of-thought, number sequences, and so on. In EM these two are entangled: the misalignment lives *in* the semantics of the insecure code itself, so the trait and its channel can't be cleanly pulled apart.

This raises a natural question: do the *semantics* of the finetuning data drive EM? The easy answer is yes — but that's too quick, because it invites a sharper one. Could we take a channel that has been filtered and scrubbed of every explicit trace of the trait, and have it *still* carry that trait? This is where Subliminal Learning (SL) comes in. <d-cite key="cloud2025subliminallearninglanguagemodels"></d-cite> shows the answer is yes: a trait can transmit through a channel cleaned of any semantic reference to it. Importantly, SL is not the antithesis of EM/WG neither is it an extension of it. Rather it sits *inside* that picture, offering a partial account of what may be driving the transfer in the first place.
